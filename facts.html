Facts And News

1. Each year, approximately 8 million metric tons of plastic waste enter the oceans, harming marine life and ecosystems.

2. The average person generates about 4.4 pounds (2 kg) of waste per day, with a significant portion being recyclable materials.

3. Recycling one ton of paper can save 17 trees, 7,000 gallons of water, and 463 gallons of oil.

4. E-waste is the fastest-growing waste stream globally, with an estimated 50 million metric tons generated annually.

5. Composting organic waste can reduce landfill waste by up to 30% and produce nutrient-rich soil for gardening and agriculture.

RISHIKESH

Air Quality: Moderate (AQI ≈ 128); PM₂.₅ ≈ 70 µg/m³. Water Quality: Upstream Ghats Class A; localized pollution in tourist zones. Cleanliness / Waste: Good around ashram belts; weaker in bazaar/tourist areas. Infrastructure / Government Initiatives: Solid waste plant at Shyampur; need for full SWTP and better sewage treatment. Overall: Moderately good water quality, but major improvements needed in solid waste management and sewage infrastructure to cope with tourist and pilgrimage loads. Positive Findings: Ganga River water in Rishikesh shows fairly good quality for aquatic life (DO ~10 mg/L). Baseline assessment of solid waste by Aviral Ganga Project quantifies waste and stakeholder awareness. State sanitation plan (Jan 2025) sets goals for city-wide sanitation and open-defecation elimination. Areas of Concern: Weak solid-waste management: no segregation at source, inadequate collection, open dumping. Sewage treatment capacity issues: Lakkarghat STP designed for 6 MLD, receives ~16 MLD; defunct STP poses environmental risk. Urban/tributary stretches (e.g., Chandrabhaga River) show poor water quality in some samples (DO as low as 0.4 mg/L). Plastic and litter issues during tourist/pilgrimage influxes remain a problem. Key Reports / Data Sources: Water Quality Index of Ganga River Water, Rishikesh, Uttarakhand, India. Assessment of water quality and suitability analysis of River Ganga at Rishikesh. DEHRADUN

Air Quality: Average AQI ≈ 140–150 (moderate to poor); PM₁₀ ≈ 155 µg/m³, PM₂.₅ ≈ 88 µg/m³. Winter smog and construction dust persist. ✅ Positive: City improved from 37th → 19th in Clean Air Survey 2025 due to tree-planting, e-vehicles, and mechanised sweeping (TOI). Water Quality: Mostly Class A–B, but Rispana and Bindal streams still carry sewage and garbage. ✅ Positive: STP upgrades and monitoring are underway to improve river health (TOI, The Week). ❌ Negative: Pollution, encroachments, and untreated discharge continue to affect ecology, groundwater, and public health (TOI, Moneycontrol). How Clean is Dehradun Overall? Dehradun’s cleanliness level is moderate and improving, but far from ideal. Door-to-door collection and monitoring are better than before, yet segregation, waste processing, and water-stream cleanliness remain weak. Continuous construction, tourism pressure, and poor waste segregation keep the city in the “developing but not clean” bracket. Positive Developments Vacuum-based road sweepers introduced — first in Uttarakhand — to reduce roadside dust (Times of India, Apr 2025) Digital Deposit Refund System (DDRS) launched by the state to reduce plastic pollution (Economic Times Government, 2025) Citizen & NGO participation increasing through local clean-up and plastic-bank projects Air-quality monitoring network expanded under Smart City and NCAP programmes Negative Reports Garbage hotspots reappearing in many wards despite cleanup claims; DMC plans drone-based monitoring (Times of India, Oct 2025 — “Garbage hotspots reappear in Doon …”) CAG report found major shortcomings: only ≈ 3 % of waste segregated at source; several vehicles uncovered; open dumping still common (Times of India, Mar 2025 — “CAG report reveals major shortcomings in waste management …”) “Dustbin-free city” initiative backfired, leading to open littering in public spaces (Times of India, Feb 2025 — “Dustbin-free initiative failure …”) Kargi transfer station repeatedly overloaded, especially post-festival seasons (Times of India, Nov 2024 — “Dehradun’s Kargi transfer station flooded with 100 tonnes of post-Diwali garbage”)

KASHIPUR

Air Quality: Moderate to poor; industrial dust PM₁₀ ≈ 135 µg/m³; intermittent SO₂ spikes near industrial corridors. Water Quality: Class B–C; effluent from small industrial units detected. Infrastructure / Government Initiatives: Online stack monitoring at major plants; river‑rejuvenation plans for Bhela and Dhela rivers; master plan includes industrial zoning, sanitation and environmental safeguards. Overall: Industrial activity drives economic growth but contributes to air and water pollution; solid-waste and sanitation systems need improvement. Positive Findings: Awareness of solid-waste and pollution issues is documented in recent studies. Some improvement in municipal sanitation and waste collection under Swachh Survekshan 2024. Online monitoring of industrial emissions implemented. Areas of Concern: Poor air quality in industrial zones; frequent PM and SO₂ spikes. Water contamination from industrial effluents; some river stretches severely polluted. Solid-waste management unsound: no segregation, inadequate collection, open dumping. Government / Initiative Actions: CEPI / industrial pollution action plans. Investments in STP/FSTP for wastewater management. Enhanced waste collection campaigns and public awareness initiatives. What Can Be Done: Strengthen industrial emissions monitoring and enforce pollution control. Improve air-quality management and dust suppression. Upgrade wastewater and river treatment capacity. Implement solid-waste segregation, collection, and treatment. Enforce industrial zoning and buffer zones. Conduct public-awareness campaigns and riverfront cleanliness drives. Strengthen monitoring, transparency, and enforcement by pollution-control authorities. NAINITAL

Air Quality: Good to satisfactory (PM₁₀ ≈ 80 µg/m³). Water Quality: Class A; Naini Lake regularly monitored. Cleanliness / Waste: Generally good but crowded in peak tourist months; seasonal plastic overflow from tourists. Infrastructure / Government Initiatives: Lake‑cleaning programme ongoing; monitoring of water level and lake inflow recharge under active review. Overall: The hill‑station environment remains relatively strong, especially in water and air quality, but tourism stress, seasonal plastic waste and lake‑recharge vulnerability remain significant challenges. Positive Findings: Lake‑cleaning efforts underway and lake maintenance remains a priority. Air quality in many periods remains in the “good to satisfactory” range despite tourist influx. Tourist economy recognised environment‑challenge, leading to monitoring and management focus. Areas of Concern: Water level of Naini Lake has dropped to a 5‑year low, raising water scarcity and recharge concerns. Cleanliness rankings show cantonment area still among the worst in state; source segregation and waste‑processing metrics remain weak. Tourist season causes plastic overflow and waste‑management pressure; untreated sewage and runoff are contaminating the lake recharge zones. Government / Initiative Actions: Pollution‑control tribunal (NGT) ordered probe into untreated sewage and garbage discharge into Naini Lake. Urban solid‑waste audit (CAG) revealed major shortcomings in waste‑management in ULBs including Nainital district. Lake‑recharge and watershed studies identify unplanned construction, encroachments and catchment‑area threats. What Can Be Done: Strengthen waste and sewage management, especially during peak tourist months. Protect lake‑recharge areas and prevent encroachments. Maintain air quality with vehicle and dust control measures. Improve cleanliness and enforce source segregation. Manage tourist waste via awareness campaigns and additional collection infrastructure. Key Reports / Resources: “NGT orders probe into Naini lake water pollution” – 2025. “Lake in Nainital hits 5‑year‑low, sparks water scarcity fears” – 2025. “Swachh Survekshan 2024: Landour slips amid tourist surge, Nainital still dirtiest cantonment in state”. “CAG report reveals ‘major shortcomings’ in waste management” – urban bodies Uttarakhand. “Uttarakhand Ambient Air Quality Characteristics” – annual summary 2025.
